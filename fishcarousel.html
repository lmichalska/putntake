Rainbow Trout

The rainbow trout is named for the red stripe that runs along its side. It has a sleek body with a shiny silver color that turns into a light pink stripe down its side. The back is a darker olive-green with tiny black spots that go over its back fin and tail.


Max. weight: 12 kg
Max. length: 80 cm

How to catch?

Start by using light to medium tackle to feel the trout’s light bite. Bait such as worms, corn, or small dough baits are highly effective, especially when fished near the bottom or floated under a bobber. For lure fishing, small spinners or spoons that mimic small fish can attract trout.


The rainbow trout primarily feeds on amphipods, snails, leeches, flies, grasshoppers, beetles, small crustaceans, and insect larvae. Large rainbow trout also feed on small fish.

Danish Golden Trout

Danish golden trout feature a vibrant golden-yellow to deep orange color, adorned with scattered black spots across their body. They're distinguished by a prominent red to orange stripe running along their sides.


Max. weight: 4 kg
Max. length: 30 cm

How to catch?

Utilize lightweight fishing tackle, including light spinning or fly rods with small reels and line, and experiment with baits or lures mimicking their natural prey like small spinners, flies, or worms. Present your bait naturally, avoiding startling the fish, and pay attention to ideal fishing times, typically during early morning or late afternoon when insect activity is high. Be patient, observant, and practice responsible catch and release to conserve these populations.


The diet of Danish golden trout primarily consists of a variety of aquatic insects, crustaceans, and small fish found within their freshwater habitats.

Brook Trout

Brook trout are visually striking fish, notable for their beautiful coloring and patterns. They typically have dark green to brown bodies with a distinctive marbled pattern of lighter shades running along their backs. Their sides are adorned with yellow spots interspersed among smaller red spots, each surrounded by a blue halo. The belly can range from white to orange, particularly vivid in males during the spawning season. The fins are often edged with a white leading line followed by a darker color, adding to their striking appearance in water.


Max. weight: 2.7 kg
Max. length: 64 cm

How to catch?

Opt for ultra-light tackle to enhance the sensitivity and allow for delicate presentations, which are essential in the clear waters where these fish are often found. Small worms, insects, and artificial flies are top choices for bait; brook trout are especially drawn to natural-looking presentations.


Brook trout primarily feed on a variety of aquatic and terrestrial insects, small fish, crustaceans, and other invertebrates. They are particularly fond of larvae, such as mayflies and caddisflies, as well as adult forms of these insects. As they grow larger, brook trout increasingly prey on smaller fish and occasionally on amphibian larvae like tadpoles.

River Trout

The red-spotted river trout naturally occurs in most clean streams in Denmark. The river trout is the resident form of the trout species, thus biologically the same as sea trout and brown trout. The river trout is relatively slow-growing, and therefore cannot withstand heavy fishing pressure. As a result, more and more anglers are practicing catch and release with the red-spotted river trout, ensuring there are fish to catch next time. Fish over a kilogram are rare. There is only one fish per holding spot, and even larger streams can easily be completely fished out. This has happened in many places.


Max. weight: 5 kg
Max. length: 80 cm

How to catch?

The river trout can be caught using bait fishing, spinning, or fly fishing. Bait fishers typically use worms, which are fished on a loose line in smaller streams and with a float and lead in larger streams. Always use a hook large enough—typically 1/0 or larger—to prevent undersized fish from swallowing the hook. Spin fishers prefer using small 3-7 gram spinners fished upstream through the weed beds—an effective method in the summer months—or floating 5-7 cm long wobblers fished downstream under overhanging branches and trees or undercut banks. Fly fishers can use wet flies, streamers, dry flies, or nymphs fished in the classical manner.


Depending on size and habitat, they feed mainly on insects and larvae, small baitfish (such as European bullhead), small crustaceans, snails and other water animals. Cannibalism has also been frequently observed among river trout. They are fast-swimming pursuit predators, but in faster-flowing rivers and streams, they are usually opportunistic and will take prey that is floating past by the current.

Tiger Trout

Its underlying color is brown along the back with golden yellow to orange coloration along the sides. Tiger trout often exhibit yellow vermiculation along the sides and back somewhat similar to that of brook trout—but much more vivid. However, tiger trout lack the iconic blue halo spots found on most adult brook trout.


Max. weight: 4.5 kg
Max. length: 61 cm

How to catch?

Tiger trout can be caught using bait fishing, spinning, and fly fishing. For bait fishing, use nightcrawlers or minnows with a loose line in small streams or a float in larger waters. Use size 1/0 or larger hooks. In spinning, use 3-7 gram spinners upstream through weed beds, or 5-7 cm crankbaits downstream near overhanging branches. Fly fishing with wet flies, streamers, dry flies, and nymphs works well, depending on water depth and fish activity.


Tiger Trout prefer clear, cool, well-oxygenated streams. They feed on aquatic and terrestrial insects and small fishes.

Arctic Char

The Arctic char is closely related to both salmon and lake trout, and has many characteristics of both. The fish is highly variable in color, depending on the time of year and the environmental conditions of the lake where it lives. The appearance of Arctic char differs between populations. The dorsal side of the Arctic char is dark in its color while the ventral varies from red, yellow, and white.


Max. weight: 4.5 kg
Max. length: 40 cm

How to catch?

Arctic chars respond to the same tackle used for salmon and trout. Anglers who fly fish for Arctic char should use bright-colored lures, such as streamer imitations, beads, leech and smolt imitations along with dry flies. Spin casters should use an 8-9 foot rod with fast-spinning ability


Arctic char eat shrimp, insect larvae, snails, clams and some small fish. Fish that are low on the food chain (like Arctic char) tend to have low levels of mercury. Although landlocked char tend to have higher levels of mercury than searun char, both types are safe to eat.
